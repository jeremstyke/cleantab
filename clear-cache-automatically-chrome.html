How to Clear Cache Automatically in Chrome (Without Doing It Manually Every Time)

Updated July 20265 min read

Every guide online tells you the same thing: press Ctrl+Shift+Del, tick some boxes, click Clear. That works — once. But if you're looking this up for the third time this month, you don't want instructions. You want it to stop being your job.

Here's the honest situation: Chrome has no built-in setting to clear your cache on a schedule. There's no "clear every day at 6pm" option hiding in the settings. People search for it constantly, and it simply doesn't exist.

What does exist are three workarounds. They're genuinely different, and which one fits depends on why you're clearing in the first place.

Option 1: Chrome's "clear on exit" setting

This one is built in, and most people don't know about it. Chrome can delete cookies and site data every time you close the browser completely.

  1. Open chrome://settings/content/cookies
  2. Find "Delete cookies and site data when you close all windows"
  3. Turn it on

The catch: this clears cookies and site data, not your cache. If your goal is fixing a stubborn page that won't refresh, this won't help. And it only fires when you close every Chrome window — if you keep one open for days, it never runs.

It's also all-or-nothing. You'll be logged out of everything, every time. There's an exception list, which helps, but you have to build it manually.

Option 2: Make it a habit (and use the shortcut)

Not glamorous, but worth stating: Ctrl+Shift+Del opens the delete dialog directly. On Mac, Cmd+Shift+Del.

Chrome remembers your last selection, so after the first setup it's about three seconds. If you only clear occasionally — say when a site misbehaves — this is fine and you don't need anything else.

It stops being fine when you want it to happen reliably, or when you want it to happen without thinking about it at all.

Option 3: An extension that actually schedules it

This is the only route to real automation. An extension can run on a timer — every hour, once a day, at browser startup — and clear exactly what you choose.

The reason this matters more than it sounds: automation changes what you can safely clear. When clearing is manual, you hesitate to wipe cookies because logging back into everything is annoying. When it's automatic and you've whitelisted the five sites you care about, you can wipe everything else aggressively without ever noticing.

One thing worth knowing before you automate

Cache exists for a reason. It's why sites load fast on the second visit. If you clear it every hour, you're re-downloading the same images and scripts all day — slower browsing and more data used.

For most people, clearing cache daily is already more than enough. Cookies and history are a different story: those are about privacy, not performance, and clearing them often costs you nothing except logins you've whitelisted anyway.

Automate the parts that are about privacy. Be a bit more relaxed about the cache.
